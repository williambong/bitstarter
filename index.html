Virtual rehabilitation is an alternative method to help rehabilitate stroke patients. This virtual rehabilitation 3D simulation is custom developed to focus on functional movements of the hand and upper limb function. To maintain high patient motivation, the arm movements are embedded in activities of daily living (ADL) or entertaining tasks, integrated with external sensors and devices such as a low cost virtual reality data glove with finger bending.

The patient (player) perspective focuses primarily on the specific ADL. The system leverages the flexibility and capabilities of a modern production quality 3D game engine and its associated sub-modules such as the physics engine to create real-time realistic scenarios (ADLs).

This system design is supported by evidence from neuroscience (see FAQ). The customer benefits include:

  • Complements conventional stroke rehabilitation therapy.
  • Elderly person’s and stroke patient’s physical mobility improved by appropriate physical functional movements.
  • 3D serious game to focus on functional movements of the hand and upper limb function.

Potential applications of this technology may be effective in the following industries:

  • Assistive technology and rehabilitation
  • For stroke patients who have limited mobility of the upper limbs in particular.
  • Geriatric-related care provider
  • For day rehabilitation centers of acute care hospitals and community hospitals.

Market trends and opportunities

Rate of stroke is predicted to rise 2-fold in next decade – increasing demands for expensive resources of hospital and day rehabilitation services. A problem with traditional stroke rehabilitation therapy is that patients can find stretching and lifting exercises painful, possibly finding them dull and repetitive as well (therefore, loosing motivation to do them). Weak and frail elderly or physically impaired person's lack of exercise could be improved by appropriate physical functional movements.

FAQ

I don't believe you. What is the scientific or medical evidence for this?
Neuroscience provides evidence that the control and visualization of a virtual upper limb and hand helps a stroke patient to activate the cortical network involving motor cortex and premotor cortex of the human brain. After a stroke, the brain is partially damaged. However, stroke patients can still observe and imagine actions but may have difficulties executing actions.
